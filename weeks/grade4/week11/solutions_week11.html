Week 11 — Answers & Solutions

Grade 4 · Math Challenge
Problem 1
How many of the shapes are triangles?
Answer: 4 triangles
Pretend all 10 shapes are triangles — that would be 10 × 3 = 30 corners. But there are 36, which is 6 corners too few. Each quadrilateral has 1 more corner than a triangle, so we turn 6 of the triangles into quadrilaterals. That leaves 6 quadrilaterals and 4 triangles.
Problem 2
What is the three-digit number?
Answer: 201
For three different digits to add up to 3, they can only be 0, 1 and 2. The arrangements that make a three-digit number are 102, 120, 201 and 210 — and the only odd one is 201.
Problem 3
Which element comes in place of the question mark?
Answer: 6
Look at how the shapes are arranged. Going across a row the pattern is: single, two overlapping, two spaced apart. Going down, the rows add a second copy vertically. So the missing cell needs four circles spaced apart both across and down — a 2×2 of separate circles, which is option 6.
Problem 4
Is Mark a truth-teller or a liar?
Answer: Liar
Nobody on the island can ever say “I am a liar”: a truth-teller wouldn't lie about it, and a liar wouldn't admit it. So the neighbor could never have said that — which means Mark made it up. Mark is a liar.
Problem 5
What number is missing on the scales?
Answer: 75
From the first scale, one rhombus + one square = 50 (since two of each balance 100). On the second scale the rhombuses cancel from both sides, leaving one circle = 25. So a square + a rhombus + a circle = 50 + 25 = 75.
Problem 6
In which figure is exactly half shaded?
Answer: B
Work out the shaded fraction of each figure: A = ⅓, B = ½, C = ¾, D = ¼, E = ⅖. Only the triangle B has exactly one half shaded.
